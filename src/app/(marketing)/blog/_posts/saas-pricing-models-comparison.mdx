Last month I watched a founder charge $9/month for a tool that saved enterprise teams 20 hours a week. His churn was 12% monthly. His MRR was $4,200 -- and falling. The problem wasn't the product. It was the price.

Choosing the wrong pricing model is the silent killer of SaaS startups. You can have the best product in the world, but if you're pricing it wrong, you'll never grow past a handful of paying customers. Let's break down the four pricing models that actually work -- and which one fits your business.

## Why Your Pricing Model Matters More Than Your Features

Your pricing model isn't just a number on a page. It's a **signal** to the market about who you serve, how you scale, and what kind of business you're building.

The right model means:
- Customers understand value instantly
- Your MRR grows naturally with customer success
- Revenue forecasting becomes predictable
- You capture expansion revenue without raising prices

The wrong model means churn, confusion, and a fundraising deck that doesn't add up.

## Model 1: Flat-Rate Pricing

One product. One price. No complexity.

**How it works:** You charge a single monthly or annual price for access to everything. No tiers, no add-ons, no surprises.

**Revenue example:**
- 200 customers x $49/month = **$9,800 MRR**
- Growth: add 30 customers/month -> MRR grows by $1,470/month

**When it works:**
- Your product serves a **narrow use case** (project management for freelancers, email for creators)
- You want **maximum simplicity** in sales and support
- You're early-stage and need to validate before optimizing

**When it fails:**
- Your market has wildly different willingness to pay (solopreneurs vs. enterprises)
- You're leaving money on the table from power users who'd pay 10x

**Real-world example:** Basecamp charges $99/month flat -- regardless of team size. Simple, predictable, no surprises.

## Model 2: Tiered Pricing

Multiple plans at different price points. The most common model for SaaS between $1M-$10M ARR.

**How it works:** You create 3-4 tiers (Starter, Pro, Business, Enterprise) with progressively more features, limits, or support.

**Revenue example:**
| Tier | Price | Customers | MRR |
|------|-------|-----------|-----|
| Starter | $29/mo | 150 | $4,350 |
| Pro | $79/mo | 80 | $6,320 |
| Business | $199/mo | 25 | $4,975 |
| **Total** | | **255** | **$15,645** |

**Why it works:** You **capture value across segments**. The solo founder pays $29. The growing team pays $79. The scaling company pays $199. Everyone gets the right fit.

**The key insight:** Most SaaS companies find that 60-70% of revenue comes from the **middle tier**. Your Pro plan is your money maker -- make sure it delivers real value.

**Common mistake:** Too many tiers. Five pricing pages causes decision paralysis. **Stick to 3-4 tiers maximum.**

## Model 3: Per-Seat (Per-User) Pricing

Charge per team member. The more people who use it, the more you earn.

**How it works:** Each "seat" (user account) costs a fixed monthly fee. Companies pay more as they add team members.

**Revenue example:**
- 50 companies, average 12 seats each, $15/seat/month
- 50 x 12 x $15 = **$9,000 MRR**
- Company adds 5 new hires -> **$750 expansion MRR** automatically

**Why it works:** Revenue scales with customer growth. When your customers hire more people, your MRR grows -- without you doing anything.

**The compounding effect:** This model is powerful for **net revenue retention (NRR)**. Companies like Slack and Notion see NRR above 120% because existing customers keep adding seats.

**When it fails:**
- Your product's value doesn't correlate with team size (a design tool used by one person in a 500-person company)
- Customers share logins to avoid paying per seat
- Companies downsize and you lose MRR overnight

**Real-world example:** Slack charges $7.25/user/month (Pro plan). A 20-person team pays $145/month. A 200-person team pays $1,450/month. Natural expansion built into the model.

## Model 4: Usage-Based Pricing

Customers pay for what they consume. API calls, storage, transactions, emails sent.

**How it works:** You set a price per unit of usage. Customers pay proportionally to how much they use.

**Revenue example:**
- Pricing: $0.002 per API call
- Customer A: 500K calls/month -> $1,000/month
- Customer B: 50K calls/month -> $100/month
- 100 customers, average 200K calls -> **$40,000 MRR**

**Why it works:** Perfect **value alignment**. Customers who get more value pay more. Customers who use less pay less. No one feels ripped off.

**The expansion story:** Usage-based pricing has the highest revenue ceiling. Your best customers can spend $10,000+/month while small customers pay $50. There's no artificial cap.

**When it fails:**
- Customers can't predict their bill -> anxiety and churn
- You can't forecast MRR reliably -> investors get nervous
- Your cost of goods sold (COGS) is high -> margins get squeezed

**Real-world example:** Stripe charges 2.9% + 30c per transaction. A startup processing $10K/month pays ~$320. An enterprise processing $10M/month pays ~$293,000. Same model, wildly different revenue per customer.

## Hybrid Models: The Best of Both Worlds

Most successful SaaS companies in 2026 use a **hybrid approach**:

- **Tiered + Per-Seat:** Base price per tier, plus per-seat pricing within each tier
- **Tiered + Usage:** Base subscription with usage overages (e.g., 10,000 API calls included, $0.001 per extra)
- **Per-Seat + Usage:** Flat per-seat price with add-on usage billing for premium features

**Example:** A project management tool might charge:
- Starter: $29/month (up to 5 users)
- Pro: $79/month (up to 20 users, +$4/extra user)
- Usage: $0.10 per automated workflow run

This captures value from both team size AND consumption.

## Comparison Table

| Model | Predictability | Expansion Potential | Complexity | Best For |
|-------|---------------|-------------------|------------|----------|
| **Flat-Rate** | High | Low | Low | Simple products, early-stage |
| **Tiered** | Medium-High | Medium | Medium | Multi-segment SaaS |
| **Per-Seat** | High | High | Low | Collaboration tools |
| **Usage-Based** | Low | Very High | High | APIs, infrastructure, AI |

## How to Choose Your Model

Ask yourself these questions:

1. **Does value scale with team size?** -> Per-seat
2. **Does value scale with consumption?** -> Usage-based
3. **Do you serve 3+ distinct customer segments?** -> Tiered
4. **Is your product dead-simple?** -> Flat-rate
5. **Want the best of everything?** -> Hybrid

**The honest answer:** Most SaaS startups should start with **tiered pricing** (3 plans) and evolve toward a hybrid model as they scale.
