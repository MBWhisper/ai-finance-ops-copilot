Most SaaS founders think about cash flow too late — usually when they're three months from zero. A proper cash flow forecast doesn't just tell you when you'll run out of money. It tells you exactly which growth rate you need to avoid it, how much you can safely hire, and when to raise your next round.

This guide covers the SaaS-specific cash flow model, the inputs you need, and how to build a 12-month forecast that holds up under pressure.

## Why SaaS Cash Flow Forecasting Is Different

Traditional businesses forecast cash flow by projecting sales and subtracting costs. SaaS has a structural twist: **revenue is both predictable and delayed.**

Predictable because MRR compounds. Delayed because acquiring a customer today costs cash now (sales, marketing, infrastructure) but returns revenue over months or years. This means two SaaS companies with identical MRR can have radically different cash positions depending on:

- How fast they're growing (higher growth = more upfront CAC burning cash)
- Annual vs monthly billing mix (annual plans give you cash now, reduce runway math complexity)
- Gross margin (infrastructure-heavy products have worse cash conversion)
- Payback period (how long before a new customer's cumulative payments exceed their acquisition cost)

A good SaaS cash flow forecast models all four of these dynamics explicitly.

## The SaaS Cash Flow Model: 5 Key Inputs

### Input 1: Current MRR and MRR Growth Rate

Your MRR trend is the foundation. Use a 3-month rolling average growth rate, not your best month. If MRR has been growing at 7% MoM for the last 3 months, use 7% — not the 15% month you had when you launched a promotion.

Also model a bear case (half your current growth rate) and a bull case (1.5x). Cash flow forecasts should show a range, not a single line.

### Input 2: Monthly Burn Rate

Total cash out per month: salaries, infrastructure, tools, marketing spend, office, and everything else. Separate fixed burn (doesn't change with revenue) from variable burn (grows with revenue, like infrastructure or payment processing fees).

Most early-stage SaaS companies have 80-90% fixed burn. This is actually good for forecasting — it means burn is predictable even as revenue fluctuates.

### Input 3: Gross Margin

SaaS gross margin varies widely: pure software products run 85-90%, API-heavy or AI-powered products might be 60-70% due to compute costs. Your gross margin determines how much of each new MRR dollar actually flows to covering fixed costs and eventually profit.

Formula: Gross MRR - Infrastructure/COGS = Gross Profit MRR

### Input 4: Annual Plan Mix

If 30% of your customers are on annual plans, you receive 12 months of cash upfront from new annual subscribers. This dramatically changes your cash position versus a pure monthly billing model. Model annual cash receipts as a lump sum in the month they occur, then recognize them as monthly MRR in your revenue model.

### Input 5: Churn Rate

Monthly churn directly reduces future MRR. In a cash flow forecast, churn matters because it erodes the revenue base you're projecting forward. A 3% monthly churn means you lose 30% of your MRR base per year — your forecast has to account for that constant erosion.

## Building Your 12-Month Forecast: Step by Step

### Step 1: Project MRR Forward

Start with current MRR. Apply your growth rate month-by-month, but subtract churn:

```
MRR(month N+1) = MRR(month N) × (1 + growth_rate) × (1 - churn_rate)
```

For a more sophisticated model, break this into new MRR, expansion MRR, contraction MRR, and churned MRR separately. This lets you stress-test individual assumptions.

### Step 2: Convert MRR to Cash Receipts

This is where SaaS gets different from simple revenue projection:

- Monthly subscribers pay in the month they subscribe
- Annual subscribers pay 12 months upfront in month 1, then renew in month 13
- Accounts receivable (B2B invoicing) adds 30-60 days to cash collection

Model cash receipts separately from recognized MRR. They diverge significantly when your annual plan mix changes.

### Step 3: Project Monthly Expenses

Map every expense line item month by month. For the next 3 months, use known costs. For months 4-12, project:

- Headcount: when do you plan to hire, what's the fully-loaded cost?
- Infrastructure: typically grows at 50-70% of MRR growth rate for pure SaaS
- Marketing: if you increase spend, model the revenue impact with a lag (usually 30-90 days)

### Step 4: Calculate Monthly Cash Position

```
Cash(month N+1) = Cash(month N) + Cash Receipts(N) - Total Expenses(N)
```

Run this for all 12 months in your base, bear, and bull case scenarios.

### Step 5: Identify Your Runway

Runway = the month in any scenario where Cash(N) first goes negative.

You want at least 12 months of runway in your base case and at least 6 months in your bear case at all times. If your bear case puts you below 6 months within the forecast window, you need to either cut burn or raise capital now — before it becomes urgent.

## Common Forecasting Mistakes

**Using best-month growth as the base case.** Every founder had a great month once. Your base case should be your average, not your best.

**Ignoring churn in MRR projections.** A 2% monthly churn means your MRR is constantly eroding. Many forecasts apply a growth rate without subtracting churn — this overstates future revenue by 20-40% over 12 months.

**Not modeling hiring decisions explicitly.** Salaries are usually 60-70% of SaaS burn. If you're planning to hire two engineers in month 4, that's $30-50K/month in new fixed burn — it needs to be in the model.

**Confusing revenue with cash.** If you have annual customers, you received their cash in month 1 even though you recognize it monthly. And if you have net-30 invoicing terms, your cash lags your recognized revenue by a month. Both matter for your actual cash position.

## The Real Value of Cash Flow Forecasting

The point of a cash flow forecast isn't to predict the future perfectly. It's to know which decisions change your trajectory. "If we hire one more engineer, what does that do to our runway?" "If churn doubles next quarter, when do we run out of money?" "What MRR growth rate do we need to be default alive by month 8?"

These questions are unanswerable without a model. With one, you can make hiring, fundraising, and pricing decisions with actual data instead of instinct.
