If you're a SaaS founder running on Stripe, your most important financial dashboard isn't your bank account — it's your MRR dashboard. Yet most founders either don't have one, or they're relying on Stripe's built-in reporting, which is famously inaccurate for anything beyond the simplest subscription setup.

This guide walks through exactly what a Stripe MRR dashboard should show, what metrics matter most, and how to get one running without hiring an engineer or writing SQL.

## What Is an MRR Dashboard and Why Do You Need One?

An MRR (Monthly Recurring Revenue) dashboard is a real-time view of your subscription revenue — not just the total number, but how it's changing and why. It answers the questions that determine whether your business is healthy:

- Is total MRR growing or shrinking?
- How much new revenue did you add from new customers?
- Are existing customers upgrading or downgrading?
- How much MRR did you lose to cancellations?
- What's your net revenue retention — the single most important SaaS health metric?

Stripe's default dashboard shows you total revenue and subscription counts. It doesn't show you MRR movements, cohort retention, or NRR. That's what a dedicated MRR dashboard is for.

## The 6 Metrics Your Stripe MRR Dashboard Must Include

### 1. Total MRR

The sum of all normalized monthly revenue from active paying customers. Annual plans divided by 12. Trials excluded. Refunded invoices subtracted.

### 2. MRR Growth Rate

Month-over-month MRR change as a percentage. The benchmark for early-stage SaaS is 10-15% MoM (the "T2D3" path). If you're below 5% consistently, growth is stalling.

### 3. New MRR

Revenue from customers who subscribed for the first time this month. This is your top-of-funnel conversion signal. If new MRR is flat but total MRR is growing, you're relying on expansion — which is good, but not sustainable alone.

### 4. Expansion MRR

Additional revenue from existing customers who upgraded to a higher plan. Strong expansion MRR is the hallmark of product-market fit — customers are getting enough value to pay you more.

### 5. Churned MRR

Revenue lost from customers who cancelled. Track this as a number (dollars churned) and as a rate (churned MRR ÷ total MRR). Industry benchmark: below 2% monthly is healthy, below 1% is excellent.

### 6. Net Revenue Retention (NRR)

NRR = (Starting MRR + Expansion - Contraction - Churn) ÷ Starting MRR × 100

NRR above 100% means your existing customers are paying you more over time — you'd grow even with zero new customer acquisition. This is the metric that defines best-in-class SaaS businesses. Anything above 120% is world-class.

## Why Stripe's Built-In MRR Is Wrong

Stripe calculates MRR from subscription objects, not paid invoices. This causes systematic errors:

**Annual plans**: Stripe's MRR widget may not correctly normalize $1,200/year to $100/month in all configurations, especially with proration or mid-cycle changes.

**Trials**: Free trial subscriptions appear in Stripe's MRR by default. They represent $0 in actual revenue.

**Discounts**: A 50% coupon for 3 months inflates MRR versus actual cash collected during the discount period.

**Multi-currency**: FX rate fluctuations move your "MRR" even when your customer base is unchanged.

For investor reporting, fundraising, or operational decisions, you need MRR calculated from actual paid invoices with correct normalization — not Stripe's estimate.

## What a Good Stripe MRR Dashboard Looks Like

A proper dashboard has three layers:

**Layer 1 — KPI bar at the top**: Total MRR, MRR growth %, New MRR, Churned MRR, NRR. Updated daily.

**Layer 2 — Trend charts**: MRR over time (24-month view), MRR waterfall (new vs expansion vs contraction vs churn stacked), Cohort retention heatmap.

**Layer 3 — Customer-level detail**: Sortable list of customers by MRR contribution, with their plan, subscription start date, and MRR movement (upgraded/downgraded/new/churned).

This structure lets you spot problems immediately: if the waterfall shows churned MRR accelerating while new MRR stays flat, you know churn is eating your growth before it compounds.
