Choosing the wrong pricing model is one of the fastest ways to kill MRR growth before it starts. This guide breaks down every major SaaS pricing model used in 2026, with honest trade-offs for each.

## Why Pricing Model = Revenue Model

Your pricing model is a signal to the market. It determines who buys, how fast they expand, and how predictable your MRR is.

## The 6 Core SaaS Pricing Models

### 1. Flat-Rate Pricing

One product. One price. One plan.

**Example:** Basecamp charges a flat $99/month regardless of users.

**Pros:**
- Extremely simple to sell and explain
- Predictable MRR from day one
- Low support overhead

**Cons:**
- You can't capture value from power users
- Hard to upsell

**Best for:** Tools with a narrow, well-defined use case.

### 2. Per-User (Seat-Based) Pricing

Charge per team member using the product.

**Example:** Slack, Notion, Linear.

**Pros:**
- MRR scales naturally as companies hire
- Straightforward to forecast

**Cons:**
- Customers hide users to save cost
- Leads to churning when companies downsize

**Best for:** Collaboration tools, project management software.

### 3. Usage-Based Pricing

Customers pay for what they actually use -- API calls, data processed, emails sent.

**Example:** Stripe (% of transaction), Twilio (per SMS), AWS (per compute hour).

**Pros:**
- Perfect alignment between value and cost
- MRR expands automatically with customer growth

**Cons:**
- Unpredictable MRR month-to-month
- Hard to forecast for financial planning

**Best for:** Infrastructure products, AI tools, communication APIs.

### 4. Tiered Pricing

Multiple plans (Starter / Pro / Business / Enterprise) with different feature sets.

**Pros:**
- Captures value across different customer segments
- Clear upsell path from tier to tier

**Cons:**
- Too many tiers causes decision paralysis
- Feature gating decisions are painful internally

**Best for:** Products serving multiple market segments. The most common model for $1M-$10M ARR SaaS companies.

### 5. Per-Feature Pricing

Customers pay a base price, then add features a la carte.

**Pros:**
- Customers only pay for what they need

**Cons:**
- Complex billing and invoicing
- Hard to predict MRR

**Best for:** Mature products with a well-understood feature hierarchy.

### 6. Freemium

Free tier with paid upgrades.

**Example:** Notion, Figma, Loom, AI Finance Ops.

**Pros:**
- Low customer acquisition cost (CAC)
- Product-led growth (PLG) loop

**Cons:**
- Conversion rates are typically 2-5%
- Requires a large user base to generate meaningful MRR

**Best for:** Products with viral loops and low marginal cost per user.

## How to Choose in 2026

| Question | Answer |
|---|---|
| Does value scale with team size? | Per-user |
| Does value scale with consumption? | Usage-based |
| Do you have 3+ customer segments? | Tiered |
| Are you building a PLG motion? | Freemium + Tiered |
| Is your product dead-simple? | Flat-rate |

Most funded SaaS in 2026 uses a **Tiered + Usage hybrid** -- a base seat price with usage overages.
