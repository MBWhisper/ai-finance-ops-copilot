Choosing the wrong pricing model is one of the fastest ways to kill MRR growth before it starts. This guide breaks down every major SaaS pricing model used in 2026, with honest trade-offs for each.

## Why Pricing Model = Revenue Model

Your pricing model isn't just a number — it's a signal to the market. It determines who buys, how fast they expand, and how predictable your MRR is. Get it wrong and you'll churn fast-growing customers who outgrow you, or leave money on the table with enterprise clients.

## The 6 Core SaaS Pricing Models

### 1. Flat-Rate Pricing

One product. One price. One plan.

**Example:** Basecamp charges a flat $99/month regardless of users.

**Pros:**
- Extremely simple to sell and explain
- Predictable MRR from day one
- Low support overhead

**Cons:**
- You can't capture value from power users
- High-value customers pay the same as low-value ones
- Hard to upsell

**Best for:** Tools with a narrow, well-defined use case and a homogeneous customer base.

---

### 2. Per-User (Seat-Based) Pricing

Charge per team member using the product.

**Example:** Slack, Notion, Linear — all charge per seat.

**Pros:**
- MRR scales naturally as companies hire
- Straightforward to forecast
- Sales and CS teams understand it immediately

**Cons:**
- Customers hide users to save cost ("seat-sharing")
- Slow-growing companies generate flat MRR
- Leads to churning when companies downsize

**Best for:** Collaboration tools, project management software, any product where team size = value.

---

### 3. Usage-Based Pricing

Customers pay for what they actually use — API calls, data processed, emails sent.

**Example:** Stripe (% of transaction), Twilio (per SMS), AWS (per compute hour).

**Pros:**
- Perfect alignment between value and cost
- Low barrier to start — small customers can afford you
- MRR expands automatically with customer growth

**Cons:**
- Unpredictable MRR month-to-month
- Hard to forecast for financial planning
- Support burden increases with usage complexity

**Best for:** Infrastructure products, AI tools, communication APIs — anything where consumption varies widely.

---

### 4. Tiered Pricing

Multiple plans (Starter / Pro / Business / Enterprise) with different feature sets.

**Example:** HubSpot, Intercom, most modern SaaS.

**Pros:**
- Captures value across different customer segments
- Clear upsell path from tier to tier
- Easy to A/B test individual tiers

**Cons:**
- Too many tiers causes decision paralysis
- Customers often pick the cheapest tier and never upgrade
- Feature gating decisions are politically painful internally

**Best for:** Products serving multiple market segments (SMB to enterprise). The most common model for $1M–$10M ARR SaaS companies.

---

### 5. Per-Feature Pricing

Customers pay a base price, then add features à la carte.

**Pros:**
- Customers only pay for what they need
- Can maximize revenue from feature-heavy power users

**Cons:**
- Complex billing and invoicing
- Creates a fragmented product experience
- Hard to predict MRR

**Best for:** Mature products with a well-understood feature hierarchy.

---

### 6. Freemium

Free tier with paid upgrades — either feature-locked or usage-limited.

**Example:** Notion, Figma, Loom, AI Finance Ops.

**Pros:**
- Low customer acquisition cost (CAC)
- Large top-of-funnel for word-of-mouth growth
- Product-led growth (PLG) loop

**Cons:**
- Free users consume infrastructure cost
- Conversion rates are typically 2–5%
- Requires a large user base to generate meaningful MRR

**Best for:** Products with viral loops, network effects, or low marginal cost per user.

---

## How to Choose in 2026

| Question | Answer → Model |
|---|---|
| Does value scale with team size? | Per-user |
| Does value scale with consumption? | Usage-based |
| Do you have 3+ customer segments? | Tiered |
| Are you building a PLG motion? | Freemium + Tiered |
| Is your product dead-simple? | Flat-rate |

Most funded SaaS in 2026 uses **Tiered + Usage hybrid** — a base seat price with usage overages.
