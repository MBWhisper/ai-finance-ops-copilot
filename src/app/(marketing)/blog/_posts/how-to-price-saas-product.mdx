Pricing is the most powerful lever in SaaS. A 1% improvement in pricing yields more profit than a 1% improvement in volume — yet most founders set prices once and never revisit them.

## Why Pricing Matters More Than You Think

Most SaaS companies undercharge by 20-50%. The research is clear:

- **Price increases of 1% typically yield 8.7% increase in profits** (McKinsey)
- **50% of companies have never tested their pricing** (OpenView)
- **The #1 reason for SaaS churn isn't product — it's perceived value vs. price**

## SaaS Pricing Models

### 1. Flat-Rate Pricing

One price, one plan. Simple but leaves money on the table.

**Best for:** Simple products with clear value, early-stage startups

**Example:**
- Starter: $29/month
- Pro: $79/month
- Enterprise: $199/month

**Pros:** Easy to understand, simple to sell
**Cons:** Doesn't capture willingness to pay, no expansion revenue

### 2. Tiered Pricing

Multiple tiers with increasing features/limits. The most common SaaS model.

**Best for:** Products with diverse customer segments

**Example:**
- Free: 100 records
- Starter ($29): 1,000 records
- Pro ($79): 10,000 records
- Enterprise ($199): Unlimited

**Pros:** Captures different willingness to pay, expansion revenue
**Cons:** Can confuse customers, requires clear value differentiation

### 3. Usage-Based Pricing

Pay for what you use. Growing in popularity.

**Best for:** Infrastructure, API, and platform products

**Example:**
- $0.001 per API call
- $0.10 per GB stored
- $0.50 per transaction

**Pros:** Scales with customer value, low barrier to entry
**Cons:** Revenue unpredictable, hard to forecast

### 4. Per-Seat Pricing

Price per user. Common in team tools.

**Best for:** Collaboration and team-based tools

**Example:**
- $10/user/month (minimum 5 users)
- Volume discounts at 50+, 100+

**Pros:** Revenue grows with customer team, predictable
**Cons:** Discourages adoption, doesn't reflect individual value

### 5. Hybrid Pricing

Combination of base price + usage. Increasingly popular.

**Best for:** Products with both fixed and variable value

**Example:**
- Base: $49/month (includes 1,000 credits)
- Additional: $0.05 per credit over limit

**Pros:** Predictable base + growth upside
**Cons:** Complex to communicate

## How to Set Your Price

### Step 1: Understand Value, Not Cost

Don't price based on your costs. Price based on the value you deliver.

**Questions to ask:**
- What problem do you solve?
- What's the cost of NOT solving it?
- How much time/money do customers save?
- What's the alternative (competitor, manual process)?

### Step 2: Research the Market

Look at competitors:
- What do they charge?
- What features are in each tier?
- Where are the gaps?

### Step 3: Define Customer Segments

Different customers have different willingness to pay:
- **Solo founders** — price-sensitive, need basics
- **Small teams** — willing to pay more for collaboration
- **Growing companies** — need advanced features
- **Enterprise** — value support and compliance

### Step 4: Create Value Metrics

Tie pricing to the metric that best captures value:

| Product Type | Value Metric |
|-------------|-------------|
| Analytics | Events tracked |
| CRM | Contacts managed |
| Email | Emails sent |
| Storage | GB used |
| API | Calls made |

### Step 5: Test and Iterate

**Pricing is never "done":**
- A/B test pricing pages
- Survey customers on willingness to pay
- Monitor conversion rates by tier
- Review annually minimum

## Real Pricing Examples

### Successful SaaS Pricing

| Company | Model | Starting Price |
|---------|-------|---------------|
| Notion | Per-seat | $10/user/month |
| Stripe | Usage-based | 2.9% + $0.30 |
| Slack | Per-seat | $7.25/user/month |
| Vercel | Usage-based | $20/month |
| Linear | Per-seat | $8/user/month |

### Key Patterns
1. **Free tier** — almost every successful SaaS has one
2. **Round numbers** — $29, $49, $99 (not $27.50)
3. **Annual discount** — 20% off for annual billing
4. **Enterprise tier** — custom pricing for large customers

## Pricing Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Pricing too low** — the most common mistake
2. **Too many tiers** — 3-4 is optimal, more confuses
3. **Not having a free tier** — hard to compete without one
4. **Copying competitors** — your value is different
5. **Never raising prices** — annual price increases are expected
6. **Hiding pricing** — transparency builds trust
7. **Not testing** — always be experimenting

## The Price Increase Playbook

When and how to raise prices:

1. **For new customers** — increase immediately, grandfather existing
2. **For existing customers** — give 30-60 days notice
3. **Annual increases** — 5-15% per year is standard
4. **Grandfathering** — keep existing customers at old price for 6-12 months
