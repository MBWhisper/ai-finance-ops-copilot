## The 6 Metrics Investors Actually Care About

Before you build anything, understand what investors are looking for. They care about six metrics. That's it.

### 1. MRR (Monthly Recurring Revenue)

**What it is:** The predictable revenue you earn each month from subscriptions.

**Why investors care:** It's the foundation of everything. No MRR = no SaaS business.

**How to present it:**
- Current MRR (e.g., $85,000)
- MRR growth rate (e.g., +12% MoM)
- MRR breakdown: New + Expansion - Contraction - Churned

**Example dashboard card:**
```
MRR: $85,000
MoM Growth: +12%
New MRR: $15,000
Expansion MRR: $4,500
Churned MRR: -$3,200
Net New MRR: $16,300
```

### 2. ARR (Annual Recurring Revenue)

**What it is:** MRR × 12. The annualized version of your recurring revenue.

**Why investors care:** It's the headline number for valuation. A $1M ARR SaaS is valued differently than a $100K ARR SaaS.

**How to present it:**
- Current ARR
- ARR growth rate (year-over-year)
- ARR trajectory (where will you be in 12 months?)

**Example:**
```
ARR: $1,020,000
YoY Growth: +140%
Projected ARR (12mo): $2,400,000
```

### 3. Churn Rate

**What it is:** The percentage of customers (or revenue) you lose each month.

**Why investors care:** High churn means you're filling a leaky bucket. Growth is pointless if you can't retain customers.

**Two types to show:**
- **Logo Churn:** % of customers lost
- **Revenue Churn (MRR Churn):** % of MRR lost

**Benchmarks:**
| Metric | Good | Average | Bad |
|--------|------|---------|-----|
| Logo Churn | <3% monthly | 3–5% | >5% |
| Revenue Churn | <2% monthly | 2–4% | >4% |

**Example:**
```
Logo Churn: 2.8% (↓ from 4.1% last quarter)
Revenue Churn: 1.9% (↓ from 3.2%)
```

### 4. LTV (Customer Lifetime Value)

**What it is:** How much revenue a customer generates over their entire lifetime.

**Formula:**
```
LTV = ARPU ÷ Revenue Churn Rate
```

**Example:**
- ARPU: $85/month
- Revenue churn: 3%
- LTV: $85 ÷ 0.03 = **$2,833**

**Why investors care:** It tells them how much each customer is worth — and whether your CAC is justified.

### 5. CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost)

**What it is:** How much you spend to acquire one new customer.

**Formula:**
```
CAC = Total Sales & Marketing Spend ÷ New Customers Acquired
```

**Example:**
- Sales & marketing spend: $45,000/month
- New customers: 90
- CAC: **$500**

**Why investors care:** If CAC > LTV, you're losing money on every customer. If CAC is too high relative to LTV, you can't scale profitably.

### 6. NRR (Net Revenue Retention)

**What it is:** How much revenue you retain from existing customers (including expansion, contraction, and churn).

**Formula:**
```
NRR = (Starting MRR - Churned MRR - Contraction MRR + Expansion MRR) ÷ Starting MRR
```

**Example:**
- Starting MRR: $60,000
- Churned: -$1,800
- Contraction: -$900
- Expansion: +$7,200
- NRR: ($60,000 - $1,800 - $900 + $7,200) ÷ $60,000 = **107.5%**

**The magic number:** NRR above 100% means your existing customers are spending **more** over time. This is the single metric investors love most. **Top SaaS companies hit 120–130% NRR.**

### Step 3: Add Context With Trends

Investors don't just want current numbers — they want **direction**. Show:

- **6-month MRR trend** (line chart)
- **Churn rate over time** (line chart)
- **NRR by cohort** (bar chart)
- **CAC payback period** (months to recover CAC)

### Step 4: Add the LTV:CAC Ratio

This single ratio tells investors if your business model works:

| LTV:CAC Ratio | Verdict |
|---------------|---------|
| Below 1:1 | Losing money on every customer |
| 1:1 – 3:1 | Not enough margin for growth |
| 3:1 – 5:1 | Healthy — room for profit |
| 5:1+ | Excellent — you might be under-investing in growth |

**Example:**
```
LTV: $2,833 | CAC: $500 | Ratio: 5.7x ✓
```

### Step 5: Add the NRR Waterfall

Show how NRR breaks down visually:

```
Starting MRR:     $60,000
  - Churned:      -$1,800  (3.0%)
  - Contraction:  -$900    (1.5%)
  + Expansion:    +$7,200  (12.0%)
  --------------------------------
  Ending MRR:     $64,500
  NRR:            107.5%
```

This tells a story: **even though you lost some revenue, expansion more than made up for it.**

## Common Dashboard Mistakes

### 1. Too Many Metrics

Don't show 20 metrics. Investors will get lost. Stick to the 6 core metrics plus 2–3 supporting charts.

### 2. No Trend Data

A single number means nothing without context. Show direction: improving, stable, or declining?

### 3. Inconsistent Definitions

If your churn definition changes between slides, you lose credibility. Define each metric once and use it consistently.

### 4. Forgetting the Story

The best dashboards tell a narrative: "We're growing fast (MRR), retaining well (NRR), and our unit economics work (LTV:CAC)."
