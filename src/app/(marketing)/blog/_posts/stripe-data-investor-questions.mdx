Last quarter, a founder walked into a Series A pitch with $200K MRR. The investors loved the product. Then came the questions: "What's your churn?" "What's your net revenue retention?" "What's your ARPU trend?"

He pulled up Stripe's dashboard. The investors looked at each other. They passed.

The problem wasn't the numbers -- it was that Stripe's default reporting doesn't answer the questions investors actually ask. You need to extract and transform the data yourself. Here's exactly how to do that for the five questions that matter most.

## Question 1: "What's Your Churn Rate?"

This is always the first question. Investors want to know if your revenue is a leaky bucket.

### What They're Really Asking

They want **logo churn** (what % of customers cancel each month) and **revenue churn** (what % of MRR you lose each month). Most investors care more about revenue churn because it directly impacts growth.

### How to Answer from Stripe

Go to **Stripe Dashboard > Subscriptions > Canceled**. Filter by the last 30 days. Count the number of canceled subscriptions divided by total active subscriptions at the start of the month.

**Revenue churn** is trickier. You need to calculate:

```
MRR Churn = (MRR from customers who canceled this month) / (MRR at start of month) x 100
```

If your MRR was $100,000 on July 1st and you lost $5,000 from cancellations in July, your monthly revenue churn is **5%**.

> **Pro tip:** Investors love seeing a declining churn rate over time. If your churn dropped from 8% to 3% over four quarters, that's a powerful story -- make sure you can show the trend.

### What Great Looks Like

- **Logo churn:** Under 3% monthly for B2B SaaS
- **Revenue churn:** Under 2% monthly for B2B SaaS
- **Net revenue retention** (more on this next): Over 100% means you're growing even without new customers

## Question 2: "What's Your Net Revenue Retention?"

NRR is the metric that separates good SaaS companies from great ones. It tells investors whether existing customers are spending more over time -- the holy grail of SaaS.

### How to Calculate from Stripe Data

NRR accounts for expansion, contraction, and churn from your existing customer base:

```
NRR = (Starting MRR + Expansion - Contraction - Churn) / Starting MRR x 100
```

Here's how to pull each piece from Stripe:

- **Starting MRR:** Sum of all active subscription MRR on the first of the month
- **Expansion:** Customers whose subscription value increased (upgrades, seat additions)
- **Contraction:** Customers whose subscription value decreased (downgrades)
- **Churn:** Customers who canceled entirely

You can find upgrades and downgrades in **Stripe Dashboard > Payments** by filtering for invoice line items where the amount changed from the previous billing period.

### A Real Example

Imagine you start January with $80,000 MRR:

- 5 customers upgrade, adding $6,000 in new MRR
- 3 customers downgrade, losing $2,000 in MRR
- 4 customers cancel, losing $3,000 in MRR

```
NRR = ($80,000 + $6,000 - $2,000 - $3,000) / $80,000 x 100 = 101.25%
```

**Above 100%** means your existing customers are spending more than they're churning. That's what investors want to see.

### What Great Looks Like

- **Below 90%:** Red flag -- you're losing existing revenue faster than you can replace it
- **90-100%:** Decent, but you're relying on new customer acquisition to grow
- **Above 100%:** Excellent -- your base is growing organically
- **Above 120%:** Elite -- typically seen in product-led growth and enterprise SaaS

## Question 3: "What's Your Average Revenue Per User?"

ARPU tells investors how much each customer is worth on average. It's a quick proxy for pricing power and customer quality.

### How to Extract ARPU from Stripe

The formula is simple:

```
ARPU = Total MRR / Number of Active Subscribers
```

From Stripe, pull your total MRR and active subscriber count from the subscriptions tab. But investors want more than a snapshot -- they want **ARPU trends**.

Calculate ARPU month-over-month for the last 12 months. An increasing ARPU means your upsell engine is working. A flat or declining ARPU might signal pricing pressure.

### What Great Looks Like

- **B2B SaaS:** $50-$500+ ARPU is healthy
- **PLG/Consumer SaaS:** $10-$50 is typical
- **Increasing trend:** A 10-20% ARPU increase year-over-year is a strong signal to investors

## Question 4: "How Fast Are You Growing Month-Over-Month?"

Growth rate is the most scrutinized number in any pitch. Investors want to see **consistent, accelerating growth** -- not wild swings.

### The Stripe Data You Need

Pull your MRR for each of the last 12 months. Calculate the percentage change:

```
MoM Growth = (Current MRR - Previous MRR) / Previous MRR x 100
```

**Be honest about the trend.** If you grew 15% for three months and then 5% for two months, show both. Investors respect transparency and will find inconsistencies.

### What Great Looks Like

- **Seed stage:** 15-20% MoM growth
- **Series A:** 10-15% MoM growth
- **Series B:** 8-12% MoM growth
- **Consistency matters more than spikes.** Three months at 10% beats one month at 30% and two months at 3%.

## Question 5: "What's Your LTV:CAC Ratio?"

This question tells investors whether your growth is sustainable. If it costs you $100 to acquire a customer who only pays you $80 total, you're losing money on every customer.

### How to Answer Using Stripe Data

**LTV (Lifetime Value):**

```
LTV = ARPU / Monthly Revenue Churn Rate
```

If your ARPU is $100 and monthly churn is 5%, your LTV is $2,000.

**CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost):**

This typically comes from your marketing and sales spend, not Stripe directly. But if you track marketing spend alongside Stripe revenue, you can calculate:

```
CAC = Total Sales & Marketing Spend / Number of New Customers Acquired
```

**LTV:CAC Ratio:**

```
LTV:CAC = $2,000 / $500 = 4:1
```

### What Great Looks Like

- **Below 1:1:** You're losing money on every customer -- urgent problem
- **1:1 to 3:1:** Viable but tight -- room for improvement
- **3:1 to 5:1:** Healthy -- this is the sweet spot most investors look for
- **Above 5:1:** You might be under-investing in growth
