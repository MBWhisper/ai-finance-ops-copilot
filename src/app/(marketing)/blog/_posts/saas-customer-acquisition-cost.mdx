Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) is the single most important number for understanding whether your growth is sustainable. If CAC is too high, every new customer makes you poorer.

## What Is Customer Acquisition Cost?

CAC measures the total cost of acquiring a new paying customer. It includes every dollar spent on marketing and sales to land that customer.

```
CAC = Total Sales & Marketing Spend ÷ New Customers Acquired
```

## How to Calculate CAC

### Step 1: Define the Time Period

Most SaaS companies calculate CAC monthly or quarterly.

### Step 2: Sum ALL Acquisition Costs

**Marketing costs:**
- Paid advertising (Google, LinkedIn, Facebook, Twitter)
- Content marketing (writers, designers, tools)
- SEO tools and consultants
- Email marketing platform
- Marketing automation tools
- Events and webinars
- Creative production

**Sales costs:**
- Sales team salaries
- Sales commissions and bonuses
- CRM and sales tools
- Demo and meeting tools
- Travel and entertainment

### Step 3: Count New Customers

Only count customers who converted during the same period.

### Step 4: Calculate

**Example (Monthly):**
- Ad spend: $8,000
- Content marketing: $3,000
- Sales team: $12,000
- Tools: $1,500
- New customers: 45

```
CAC = ($8,000 + $3,000 + $12,000 + $1,500) ÷ 45 = $544
```

## Blended vs. Paid CAC

### Blended CAC
Includes ALL channels (organic, paid, referrals, word-of-mouth).

```
Blended CAC = Total Spend ÷ Total New Customers
```

### Paid CAC
Only includes paid acquisition channels.

```
Paid CAC = Paid Channel Spend ÷ Customers from Paid Channels
```

**Why both matter:**
- Blended shows overall efficiency
- Paid shows the true cost of bought growth
- If blended is low but paid is high, organic is doing the heavy lifting

## CAC Payback Period

How long to recover your CAC:

```
CAC Payback (months) = CAC ÷ (ARPA × Gross Margin)
```

**Example:**
- CAC: $544
- ARPA: $99/month
- Gross margin: 80%

```
Payback = $544 ÷ ($99 × 0.80) = 6.9 months
```

| Payback | Assessment |
|---------|-----------|
| < 6 months | Excellent |
| 6-12 months | Good |
| 12-18 months | Acceptable (enterprise) |
| > 18 months | Risky |

## How to Reduce CAC

### 1. Improve Conversion Rates

Small improvements in conversion dramatically reduce CAC.

**If your funnel:**
- Gets 1,000 visitors/month
- 5% sign up (50 leads)
- 20% convert to paid (10 customers)
- With $5,000 spend → CAC = $500

**After improving signup to 8%:**
- 80 leads × 20% = 16 customers
- CAC drops to $312 (38% reduction!)

### 2. Invest in Content & SEO

Content marketing has the lowest CAC over time:
- Write for keywords your ICP searches for
- Create comparison pages (vs. competitors)
- Build free tools (calculators, templates)
- Publish case studies

### 3. Build a Referral Program

Referral CAC is typically 50-80% lower than paid:
- Offer credits or discounts for referrals
- Make sharing easy (one-click)
- Reward both referrer and referee

### 4. Optimize Onboarding

Better onboarding = higher trial-to-paid conversion = lower effective CAC:
- Guide users to the "aha moment" fast
- Send activation emails
- Offer live onboarding calls for high-value trials

### 5. Raise Prices

The most overlooked CAC reduction strategy:
- Higher ARPA means the same CAC yields better LTV:CAC
- Most SaaS can increase prices 20-50% without churn impact

### 6. Focus on High-Value Segments

Not all customers are equal:
- Identify your best customers (highest LTV, lowest churn)
- Focus marketing on similar prospects
- Create segment-specific content and campaigns

## Common CAC Mistakes

1. **Not including all costs** — salaries, tools, events all count
2. **Using wrong time period** — CAC and customers must be from the same period
3. **Ignoring organic** — blended CAC masks paid CAC problems
4. **Not segmenting** — CAC varies wildly by channel and customer type
5. **Chasing low CAC at expense of quality** — cheap customers often churn fast
