Your runway is the number of months your startup can survive before it runs out of cash. It's the single most important metric for any early-stage company that's not yet profitable.

Yet most founders calculate it wrong. This guide shows you the correct formula, common mistakes, and practical ways to extend your runway without raising a new round.

## What Is Runway?

Runway is simple in theory: take the cash you have, divide by what you burn each month, and that's how many months you have left.

But the definition of "burn" varies. There are two types:

- **Gross Burn** — total monthly operating expenses (salaries, rent, software, contractors)
- **Net Burn** — gross burn minus monthly revenue

For pre-revenue startups, gross burn and net burn are the same. For post-revenue startups, net burn is what matters.

## How to Calculate Runway

The basic formula:

```
Runway (months) = Cash on Hand / Net Monthly Burn
```

### Step-by-step Example

1. You have **$180,000** in the bank
2. Monthly expenses total **$45,000**
3. Monthly revenue is **$15,000**
4. Net burn = $45,000 - $15,000 = **$30,000/month**
5. Runway = $180,000 / $30,000 = **6 months**

Six months might feel like plenty, but it takes 3–6 months to raise a round. That means you need to start fundraising almost immediately.

### Including Growth in Your Calculation

If your revenue is growing, your net burn decreases over time. A more accurate model accounts for this:

| Month | Cash | Revenue | Expenses | Net Burn | Ending Cash |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | $180K | $15K | $45K | $30K | $150K |
| 2 | $150K | $17K | $45K | $28K | $122K |
| 3 | $122K | $19K | $46K | $27K | $95K |
| 4 | $95K | $22K | $46K | $24K | $71K |
| 5 | $71K | $25K | $47K | $22K | $49K |
| 6 | $49K | $28K | $47K | $19K | $30K |

Your runway extends further as revenue grows. This is why growth is your best defense against running out of cash.

## Common Runway Calculation Mistakes

**Mistake #1: Using gross burn instead of net burn.** If you have revenue, always use net burn. Gross burn overstates your cash consumption.

**Mistake #2: Ignoring one-time expenses.** Annual software licenses, legal fees, and hardware purchases are real cash outflows. Amortize them across the months they cover.

**Mistake #3: Assuming no revenue growth.** The static model assumes flat revenue. If you're growing even 5–10% month-over-month, your runway is significantly longer than the simple formula suggests.

**Mistake #4: Not accounting for cushion.** Never plan to hit zero. Set a minimum cash threshold (3 months of expenses) below which you treat as "emergency only."

## 5 Ways to Extend Runway Without Raising Money

### 1. Reduce Non-Personnel Costs First

Review every SaaS subscription, contractor, and service. You can usually cut 15–20% without impacting product development. Cancel tools you haven't used in 30 days.

### 2. Move to Annual Billing

If you have product-market fit, offer annual plans at a discount. This converts future revenue into cash today. A 15% discount is worth it if it buys you 2–3 extra months of runway.

### 3. Hire Contractors Instead of Employees

A full-time employee costs 30–40% more than their salary (benefits, taxes, equipment). For non-core functions, contractors are cheaper and more flexible.

### 4. Extend Payment Terms

Ask your vendors for Net-60 instead of Net-30 terms. If they agree, you've effectively added a month of free float. Do this with your largest expenses first.

### 5. Pause Low-ROI Marketing Spend

If your CAC payback period is longer than 12 months, pause that channel. Focus on organic growth and high-intent channels until you extend your runway to a comfortable level.
