You built a product. People pay for it. You're a founder, not an accountant. But if you can't read your own financial model, you're flying blind.

The good news: you don't need to understand debits and credits. You need to understand three things -- and that's it.

## The Three Things That Matter

Every SaaS financial model boils down to:

1. **Money coming in** (revenue)
2. **Money going out** (expenses)
3. **What's left** (profit or loss)

That's the entire game. Everything else is detail.

## The SaaS Unit Economics Model

The most important model for a SaaS founder is unit economics -- understanding the economics of a single customer.

### Customer Lifetime Value (LTV)

How much revenue does one customer generate over their lifetime?

**Formula:**
```
LTV = Average Revenue Per Month x Average Customer Lifetime
```

**Example:**
- Average revenue per customer: $99/month
- Average customer stays 24 months
- LTV = $99 x 24 = **$2,376**

### Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC)

How much does it cost to get one customer?

**Formula:**
```
CAC = Total Sales & Marketing Spend / New Customers Acquired
```

**Example:**
- You spend $10,000/month on marketing
- You acquire 50 new customers
- CAC = $10,000 / 50 = **$200**

### The LTV:CAC Ratio

This tells you if your business is sustainable:
- **LTV:CAC > 3** -- Healthy. You're profitable on each customer.
- **LTV:CAC = 1-3** -- Marginal. You might not be profitable after expenses.
- **LTV:CAC < 1** -- You're losing money on every customer.

**Our example:** $2,376 / $200 = **11.9:1** -- Excellent.

## The Cash Flow Model

Cash flow is different from profit. You can be profitable on paper but run out of cash.

### Why This Happens in SaaS

- A customer pays $1,188 upfront for an annual plan
- Your accounting shows $99/month in revenue (correct)
- But your bank account received $1,188 this month (actual cash)
- Next month, you receive $0 from that customer (cash flow drops)

### The Simple Cash Flow Formula

```
Cash Flow = Cash In - Cash Out
```

Track this monthly:
- **Cash In:** All payments received (Stripe, PayPal, etc.)
- **Cash Out:** All expenses paid (salaries, hosting, tools)

If Cash In > Cash Out, you're cash flow positive. If not, you're burning cash.

## The Cohort Model

Cohort analysis groups customers by when they signed up and tracks each group over time.

### Why It Matters

If you acquire 100 customers in January and 100 in February, but the February cohort churns faster, your overall metrics hide the problem. Cohort analysis reveals it.

### What to Look For

- **January cohort:** 100 customers -> 85 after 3 months (85% retention)
- **February cohort:** 100 customers -> 70 after 3 months (70% retention)

The February cohort performed worse. Maybe a pricing change, a bad onboarding experience, or a seasonal effect. Without cohorts, you'd miss this.
