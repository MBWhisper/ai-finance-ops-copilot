Burn rate is the speed at which your startup spends money. It tells you how long you can survive before running out of cash — and whether you're spending efficiently.

Every founder needs to know their burn rate cold.

## What Is Burn Rate?

Burn rate measures how much cash your business loses each month. It's the difference between what you spend and what you earn.

There are two types:

### Gross Burn Rate

**Total monthly operating expenses** — regardless of revenue.

```
Gross Burn Rate = Total Monthly Operating Expenses
```

**Example:**
- Salaries: $40,000
- Rent: $3,000
- Software/tools: $2,000
- Marketing: $5,000
- Other: $2,000

```
Gross Burn = $52,000/month
```

### Net Burn Rate

**Cash lost after revenue** — the actual amount your bank account decreases each month.

```
Net Burn Rate = Monthly Operating Expenses - Monthly Revenue
```

**Example:**
- Monthly expenses: $52,000
- Monthly revenue: $15,000

```
Net Burn = $52,000 - $15,000 = $37,000/month
```

**Net burn is what matters.** It tells you exactly how fast your cash reserves are shrinking.

## How to Calculate Runway

Runway = how many months until you run out of cash.

```
Runway (months) = Cash Balance ÷ Net Burn Rate
```

**Example:**
- Cash balance: $200,000
- Net burn: $37,000/month

```
Runway = $200,000 ÷ $37,000 = 5.4 months
```

| Runway | Status |
|--------|--------|
| < 3 months | Critical — fundraise immediately |
| 3-6 months | Urgent — start fundraising now |
| 6-12 months | Healthy — plan ahead |
| 12+ months | Comfortable — focus on growth |

## Burn Rate by Stage

| Stage | Typical Monthly Burn | Target Runway |
|-------|---------------------|---------------|
| Pre-seed | $10K-$30K | 18-24 months |
| Seed | $30K-$80K | 18-24 months |
| Series A | $100K-$300K | 18-24 months |
| Series B | $300K-$1M | 18-24 months |

**The 18-24 month rule:** Always maintain at least 18 months of runway. Less than 12 months puts you in survival mode.

## Burn Multiple

How efficiently you're converting spending into growth:

```
Burn Multiple = Net Burn ÷ Net New ARR
```

| Burn Multiple | Assessment |
|--------------|-----------|
| < 1 | Exceptional — efficient growth |
| 1-2 | Great — strong efficiency |
| 2-3 | Good — acceptable |
| 3-5 | Mediocre — needs improvement |
| 5+ | Terrible — burning cash wastefully |

**Example:**
- Net burn: $37,000/month
- Net new ARR: $20,000/month

```
Burn Multiple = $37,000 ÷ $20,000 = 1.85 (great)
```

## What's a "Good" Burn Rate?

There's no universal answer — it depends on:

1. **Revenue growth rate** — high growth justifies higher burn
2. **Stage** — early-stage can burn more relative to revenue
3. **Market conditions** — in downturns, lower burn is better
4. **Path to profitability** — some businesses never need to be profitable (rare)

### The Growth-Adjusted Framework

**Healthy:** Burn rate is justified by growth rate
```
If ARR is growing 3x/year → burn multiple < 2 is healthy
If ARR is growing 2x/year → burn multiple < 1.5 is healthy
If ARR is growing < 2x/year → burn multiple < 1 is healthy
```

## How to Reduce Burn Rate

### Quick Wins (immediate impact)
1. **Cut unused subscriptions** — audit all SaaS tools monthly
2. **Renegotiate contracts** — vendors expect this
3. **Reduce cloud costs** — right-size instances, use reserved capacity
4. **Pause non-essential hiring** — hire only critical roles

### Medium-Term (1-3 months)
1. **Improve gross margins** — optimize infrastructure costs
2. **Increase prices** — most SaaS undercharges
3. **Reduce churn** — retention is cheaper than acquisition
4. **Automate manual processes** — reduce headcount needs

### Strategic (3-6 months)
1. **Shift to product-led growth** — lower CAC
2. **Build self-serve** — reduce support costs
3. **Focus on high-value customers** — better unit economics
4. **Consider usage-based pricing** — revenue scales with usage

## Common Mistakes

1. **Confusing gross and net burn** — gross burn ignores revenue, making you look worse than you are
2. **Not accounting for annual expenses** — insurance, taxes, equipment refreshes
3. **Ignoring seasonality** — Q4 revenue spikes can mask burn rate
4. **Burning to hit vanity metrics** — growth without retention is wasted money
5. **Not modeling scenarios** — always know your best case, worst case, and base case
