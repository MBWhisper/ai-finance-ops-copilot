ARR (Annual Recurring Revenue) is the most cited metric in SaaS -- but it's also one of the most frequently miscalculated. Here's the exact formula, worked examples, and the mistakes that skew your numbers.

## The ARR Formula

```
ARR = MRR x 12
```

**Example:**
- January MRR: $12,500
- ARR: $12,500 x 12 = $150,000

## Why ARR Does Not Equal Annual Revenue

| Metric | What It Measures | Time Orientation |
|---|---|---|
| ARR | Current run rate, annualized | Forward-looking |
| Annual Revenue | Cash actually received in a year | Backward-looking |
| Bookings | Value of new contracts signed | Point-in-time |

## ARR for Different Subscription Types

### Monthly Subscriptions

```
Customer A: $99/month = $1,188 ARR
Customer B: $299/month = $3,588 ARR
```

### Annual Subscriptions

Do NOT multiply by 12 -- you'd be double-counting.

```
Customer C pays $2,400/year upfront = $2,400 ARR (already annual)
```

## The 5 Most Common ARR Mistakes

1. **Including one-time revenue** -- setup fees, consulting are not recurring
2. **Using gross vs net revenue** -- always clarify which you're reporting
3. **Not normalizing annual plans** -- if a customer cancels in month 3, adjust ARR downward
4. **Including trials and freemium users** -- ARR is recurring revenue only
5. **Snapshotting ARR on the wrong date** -- measure at the same time each month

## ARR vs MRR: When to Use Each

- **Use MRR** for operational decisions -- month-to-month growth, churn analysis
- **Use ARR** for investor reporting, benchmarking, company valuation

## ARR-Based Valuation

- **Bootstrapped / pre-traction:** 2-4x ARR
- **Growing (20%+ MoM):** 5-10x ARR
- **VC-backed, high-growth:** 10-30x ARR
